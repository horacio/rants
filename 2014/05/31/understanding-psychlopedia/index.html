During the past weeks, I started to question myself about one of my current endeavors: Psychlopedia. I want to get a thorough comprehension of what I am trying to accomplish with the time and resources I am putting into this project. Know what can be done, thus find out and test what could or should be done to finally do what must be done.

No fooling around.

I want to make clear that I don't know for certain any of what I will be talking about in this post. This is an exploratory exercise to me, and it should be so for you too, so please bear that in mind.

I was reading thoughtbot's Playbook when I stumbled on Product Design Sprints, an amazing concept developed by the Google Ventures' design team. Essentially, a Product Design Sprint is a technique that increases the chance of channeling efforts into meaningful and sustainable productions. Please read thoughtbot's amazing article on this subject which explains it to the detail perfectly.

Currently I am stressing the following question: 'what job is the user hiring the product to do?'. It is a question proposed by Clayton Christensen, a Business Administration professor at Harvard Business School. Having asked myself about this not even once in my own product design path, I was really intrigued. So I grabbed my coat and went out for a random walk (on a Hidden Markov chain), my regular deep thinking session.

Walking down Colegiales, I started to identify three possible jobs Psychlopedia in its current conception could do and do pretty well:

       Feed our curiosity and increase the quality of its by-products.

       Connect us to others through our inner experiences.

       Lessen our fear and/or anxiety towards psychonautics.

So now I am trying to finish the critical path based on these assumptions.

This is a work-in-progress and part of a series on product design focused on Psychlopedia, a digital archive of psychedelic experiences.
